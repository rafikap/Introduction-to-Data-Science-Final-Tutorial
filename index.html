The Effects of Covid-19 and Online Class on Grades at the University of Maryland¶

By Raphael Kaplan¶

Motivation¶

In response to Covid-19, in mid-spring 2020, UMD moved classes online and changed grading policies. This obiously had an effect on student grades during that semester, and we will attempt to quantify that effect here. We will compare historic grade data to the various permutations of online class that occured between spring 2020 and spring 2021. A we will soon see, it is clear that grades were inflated diring this time but the reason is not quite as clear. Some suggest it is due to the option of switching courses to be graded as pass/fail during some of those semesters, some think it is due to professors being lenient during the pandemic, while others point to the ease of cheating during online schooling.

Question: What factors caused this inflation?¶

As expected, there was a huge spike when school went online for covid! Grades remained higher than average for the next three semesters although less so. The following potential reasons come to mind:

  1. UMD allowed taking a class pass/fail during some of these semesters. Students with lower grades likely opted for a pass rather than a below average grade.
  2. Students took advantage of online assessments (cheating).
  3. Teachers were more lenient during this time due to the difficulty and stresses of the pandemic.

Option 1: The pass/fail option inflated grades¶

First let's try to see how much the pass fail option increased grades.

For this analysis we will assume that if a student had a below average grade, they would opt to take the pass/fail option. Above average, they likely kept their grade as is. To test this, we can replace all of the Pass grades with a below average grade and see if this brings the average GPA for those semesters back down. So we will replace a fraction of the grades marked as Other with the 3rd quartile, or 'the average below average' GPA.

But what should we consider average grade? And how many Other grades should we replace?¶

For this we will look to historical data. For each course, we will find the historical average GPA and amount of Other grades. Since pass/fail was not an option in previous semesters, we can subtract this average from the covid Other numbers and assume these are the passes.

Next Step: Replace Other grades with the 3rd quartile historic data¶

First, a couple notes about online class and pass/fail grading during this time:

  • In Spring 2020, UMD offered an opt-out option for pass fail grading in the middle of the semester. This means that students were sent an email informing them that their grade would be counted as pass/fail unless otherwise instructed by the student. Classes moved online mid semester and finals were given online.

  • Fall 2021 had no option for pass/fail. Most classes and assesments were online.

  • In Spring 2021, UMD once again gave the option for pass/fail grading. This differed from 2020 with an opt-in model and a maximum of 7 credits per student (at UMD this is ~2 courses). Classes were beginnning to transition to in person but remained ~80 online and assesments were online.

Here we will calculate the "Normalized" GPA for the pass/fail semesters. This is calculated as follows:

GPA_Normalized = (current_other_grades - (avg_historic_pct_other_grades current_total_students)) + (current_gpa total_graded_students) / (current_other_grades - (avg_historic_pct_other_grades * current_total_students) + total_graded_students)

Option 2: Cheating¶

This one is more difficult to quantify, although we will make an attempt. The assumtion we will make here is that courses that have answers readily available on the internet and are mostly exam focused will be easier to cheat in. Courses that generally require original work without clear answers will be more dificult to cheat in.

Based on these assumptions we will analyze the grades of English, Mathematics and Computer Science.

If our hypothesis is correct, Math will have a very high jump in grades, English will have one relatively lower, and Computer Science will be somewhere in between where answers can be found online but courses tend to be project based.

Conclusion¶

As we have seen, the major spike in grades in 2020 can largely be attributed to the option to take courses pass/fail. However this is not the full story. Many professors were likely more lenient during the pandemic due to challenges of the pandemic itself and due to the dificulty of switching to online learning. The effect of this requires a deeper analysis and is beyond the scope of this project. It would be interesting to survey professors and administrators hear their thoughts on those times. The third factor we looked as was cheating. While also difficult to quantify precisely, we attempted to show that more technical departments with courses that rely on in person exams would be more effected by cheating than essay and project based courses. Our initial analysis suggests that this is the case but further analysis is required here as well.
